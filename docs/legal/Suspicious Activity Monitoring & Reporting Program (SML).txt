1. Program Objective

The Suspicious Activity Monitoring and Reporting Program (“SML”) establishes the framework used by AurumShield to:

• Detect potential money laundering
• Detect terrorist financing
• Detect sanctions evasion
• Identify financial fraud
• Identify market manipulation
• Detect illicit commodity settlement activity

The program ensures compliance with the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and associated FinCEN regulations.

Because AurumShield processes high-value corporate settlements, the monitoring framework is designed for institutional-scale transaction risk detection.

2. Fail-Closed Monitoring Architecture

AurumShield operates a Fail-Closed Compliance Model.

This means:

No transaction may settle unless the compliance monitoring engine confirms the transaction has passed all risk checks.

If a risk rule triggers:

• Transaction is automatically halted
• Funds remain in escrow
• Compliance investigation begins

3. Monitoring Scope

The monitoring program evaluates activity across:

Customer Behavior

Customer transaction patterns relative to known business activity.

Counterparty Relationships

Risk analysis of transaction counterparties.

Movement of assets through the AurumShield settlement network.

5. Suspicious Activity Detection Categories

The monitoring engine identifies suspicious activity across the following categories.

5.1 Structuring

Structuring occurs when a customer deliberately breaks a large transaction into multiple smaller transactions to avoid reporting thresholds.

Examples include:

• Multiple wire transfers slightly below reporting thresholds
• Repeated transfers just below internal monitoring limits
• Coordinated structuring across related accounts

Structuring behavior generates automatic investigation alerts.

5.2 Layering

Layering involves complex transaction patterns intended to obscure the origin of funds.

Indicators include:

• Rapid transfers between multiple entities
• Circular settlement flows
• Settlement chains with no economic purpose

Layering detection relies on transaction graph analysis.

5.3 Velocity Anomalies

Velocity monitoring detects abnormal transaction activity.

Indicators include:

• Sudden spikes in transaction size
• Rapid asset transfers across multiple counterparties
• Large settlements inconsistent with historical activity

5.4 Economic Purpose Analysis

Each transaction must demonstrate a legitimate economic purpose.

Indicators of suspicious activity include:

• Transactions inconsistent with stated business operations
• High-value settlements without commercial justification
• Repeated settlement reversals

5.5 Sanctions Evasion

Indicators include:

• Attempts to transact with sanctioned jurisdictions
• Entities linked to sanctioned persons
• Transactions routed through sanctioned intermediaries

Sanctions screening occurs through OpenSanctions integration.

5.6 Digital Asset Risk

Stablecoin settlement introduces additional AML risk.

Monitoring includes:

• Blockchain exposure analysis
• Detection of sanctioned wallet addresses
• Exposure to illicit financial networks

Wallet addresses linked to:

ransomware activity

darknet markets

sanctioned entities

trigger immediate compliance review.

5.7 Corporate Ownership Risk

Corporate entities with complex ownership structures may present elevated AML risk.

Indicators include:

• Rapid changes in beneficial ownership
• Use of shell companies
• Multi-jurisdictional ownership chains

6. Alert Generation

When suspicious indicators are detected, the monitoring engine generates a compliance alert.

Alerts are categorized by severity.

Alert Level	Action
Low	Automated review
Medium	Compliance review
High	Transaction halted
7. Compliance Investigation Procedures

Upon alert generation, the Compliance Officer initiates an investigation.

The investigation process includes:

Step 1 — Transaction Review

Examination of transaction details and customer profile.

Step 2 — Counterparty Review

Analysis of transaction counterparties.

Step 3 — Enhanced Due Diligence

Additional documentation may be requested from the customer.

Step 4 — Risk Determination

Compliance determines whether activity is suspicious.

8. Suspicious Activity Reporting (SAR)

If suspicious activity is confirmed, AurumShield files a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with FinCEN.

SAR Filing Threshold

SAR filing is required when:

• Suspicious transactions exceed $2,000
• Potential money laundering is identified
• Fraud or illicit activity is suspected

Filing Deadline

SARs must be filed within 30 days of detection.

9. SAR Confidentiality

SAR filings are strictly confidential.

Information related to SARs may not be disclosed to:

• Customers
• External parties
• Unauthorized employees

Violation of SAR confidentiality may result in disciplinary action.

10. Escalation Protocol

When suspicious activity is confirmed:

Settlement transaction is halted

Account access may be suspended

Compliance investigation continues

SAR filing determination is made

Accounts may be permanently terminated if illicit activity is confirmed.

11. Monitoring Model Governance

The AurumShield monitoring system is subject to governance procedures.

These include:

• Model documentation
• Periodic rule review
• False-positive reduction
• Regulatory audit readiness

Monitoring rules are reviewed at least annually.

12. Recordkeeping

All suspicious activity investigations are documented.

Records include:

• Alert data
• Investigation notes
• Supporting evidence
• SAR filing documentation

Records are retained for five (5) years.

13. Independent Testing

The Suspicious Activity Monitoring Program is independently tested annually.

Testing evaluates:

• Detection rule effectiveness
• Alert accuracy
• SAR filing procedures

Testing may be conducted by external compliance firms.

14. Regulatory Cooperation

AurumShield cooperates with lawful requests from:

• FinCEN
• Federal law enforcement agencies
• Banking partners
• Regulatory authorities

Information is provided in accordance with applicable law.

15. Program Review
